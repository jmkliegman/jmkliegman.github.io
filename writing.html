The Ringer

  • College athletes are only starting to get access to the mental health care they need: Inspired by ESPN personality Kate Fagan's book What Made Maddy Run, I examined the slowly expanding access NCAA student-athletes have to the mental health care they need and deserve. I relayed firsthand stories from athletes who have experienced depression and eating disorders, as well as vital information from experts in the field.

  • The new era of suicide prevention: In 2017, Facebook announced it would begin using artificial intelligence to identify potentially suicidal users earlier and offer them the resources they need to get help. I took a look and how they and other social media networks impact people experiencing suicidal thoughts, and what more can be done without invading users' privacy.

  • The future of abortion on TV: In recent years, more and more TV shows have been crafting thoughtful, understated, and sometimes even subversive plotlines about abortion. Following the inauguration of a president who poses a serious concern for proponents of reproductive rights, this representation is more important than ever. I talked to showrunners for Crazy Ex-Girlfriend and Jane the Virgin and other experts about the connection between real-life abortion-rights setbacks and onscreen story lines.

  • Pitch isn't just a fantasy: In September 2016, Fox premiered Pitch, a drama featuring Ginny Baker (Kylie Bunbury) as the first woman to break into Major League Baseball. In an time when female ballplayers are seen as freakish outliers and not the norm, the show offers girls and women an exciting look at the opportunities to come for them in the sport.

  • A quiet revolution on BoJack Horseman: TV characters don't often come out—or even think about coming out—as asexual. If BoJack lets Todd ponder his potential asexuality without shaming him or muting his warmhearted and patently absurd personality, the show will have succeeded in treating asexual people like actual human beings — a statement that should by no means feel bold in 2016, but, for Hollywood, would be a huge leap. I talked to advocates and viewers about Todd's impact on and the importance of asexual representation on TV.

The Week

  • The Simpsons shows why narcolepsy is no laughing matter: With its sensitivity, The Simpsons proves itself an anomaly in a comedic world that so often refuses to validate the experiences people with narcolepsy face on a daily basis — and the strain the disorder can put on relationships.

  • Could the Amazin' Mets actually be amazing?: The New York Mets made the 2015 playoffs, marking their first postseason appearance in nearly a decade. I describe the pain of rooting for the lovable losers.

  • How technology is making baseball better — even without robot umpires: Years of PITCHf/x technology has led to umpires adjusting their strike zones downward, which is more in line with the MLB rule book. Maybe robots will call games one day, but even with humans behind the plate, players and fans don't have it so bad.

Images & Voices of Hope

  • How one nonprofit campaign is fighting for better storytelling about war veterans: In endorsing examples of compassionate, accurate storytelling about veterans that go beyond portraying them as either troubled or heroic, 6 Certified committee members hope to highlight the depth and diversity of veterans without demanding creative control over the stories writers and producers want to tell.

  • Advice columnists reflect on a lack of diversity and inclusion in their field: Most prominent advice columnists are straight, cis, white and non-disabled. I spoke to Mallory Ortberg and Ijeoma Oluo, who run advice columns at Slate and The Establishment, respectively, about how more diversity among their ranks would better serve readers.

  • Behind-the-scenes look at Eli Saslow's reporting on a mass shooting survivor: In a Q&A, the Washington Post reporter let me in on how he empathetically chronicled the recovery of Cheyeanne Fitzgerald, a 16-year-old shot at Umpqua Community College in Roseburg, Oregon.

  • Doll Hospital, an art and literature journal on mental health, creates dialogue across a diverse community: Bethany Rose Lamont was having trouble finding the right outlet for her mental health struggles, so she started her own zine and recruited a host of diverse voices to join in.

Tampa Bay Times

  • Condo owners being forced out look to legislators for help: Legislators overlooked a 2007 loophole in Florida condominium law for many years. Finally, residents have their attention. In my third piece on the matter, I took a nuanced look at why this problem came to be and how residents are searching for answers. This story ran on page 1B.

  • Tarpon Springs crossbowmaker faces lawsuits over severed thumbs: Perusing recently filed lawsuits, I came across the same story nationwide: As a shooting enthusiast fires at a target, the string snaps and slices off part of his or her finger in the process. The crossbow industry is not regulated, so companies like Barnett Outdoors don't build thumb guards into their products.

  • Failed funding puts public housing renovations on hold in Tarpon Springs: For years, officials have promised residents of public housing complex Mango Circle that they'd soon have free tickets out of their cockroach-infested, mold-ridden apartments. But even when funding is explicitly promised, it never comes. I explain why.

PolitiFact

  • 60 percent of St. Petersburg car thefts due to keys left in car, mayor says: This is something St. Petersburg, Fla. Mayor Bill Foster said in a 2013 mayoral forum. I was skeptical of the high number he cited. After talking to crime experts and the police department, I discovered the real number is even higher. This story also ran on the front page of the Tampa Bay Times.

  • Super PAC criticizes Booker for low Newark graduation rate: A conservative super PAC criticized U.S. Senate candidate Cory Booker, D-N.J., for failing to improve Newark graduation rates even after receiving a donation from Facebook founder Mark Zuckerberg. But a look into how education funding works in Newark reveals that the situation is much more complicated. I broke down the context of the city's low graduation rate, as well as the long process of using Zuckerberg's funds to meet city and state educational goals.

  • The health care marketplaces have 'no privacy protections,' Cotton says: Rep. Tom Cotton, R-Ark., was one of many House GOP Obamacare critics. When healthcare.gov launched on Oct. 1, 2013, its glitches became a popular target. Cotton claimed there's no mechanism in place to collect sensitive consumer data, but cybersecurity experts told me otherwise.

El Nuevo Día

  • Las tendencias suicidas pueden superarse: I took an in-depth look at statistics from the past decade to determine whether there has been a trend in suicides on the island and, if so, why. I spoke to experts but also got crucial firsthand narratives from formerly suicidal individuals.

  • El empresarismo como alternativa: In a rough economy, young people have increasingly turned to starting up their own businesses. I put a voice to their struggles and successes and provided a clear picture of what resources are available for entrepreneurial help in Puerto Rico.

  • El curioso encanto de los memes: Meme culture is big everywhere, and Puerto Rico is no exception. Although various types of Internet jokes have been popular for ages, I noticed  a major gap: No big publications on the island had covered the topic. I went straight to the source and talked to meme creators, while getting the bigger picture from Know Your Meme.
